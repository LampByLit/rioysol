Riparian stewardship · non-extractive landscape work

How we protect this stretch of river

Rio y Sol works on a priority reach of the Amazon near Iquitos, Peru: water, forest, wildlife, and the people who live with all three. We treat the program as long-horizon work—build local leadership, keep learning, and measure what actually happens on the ground—not a ribbon-cutting exercise.

1. Problem framing & landscape diagnostics

Upstream pressures, weak enforcement, informal clearing, trash in the water, and a hotter, less predictable climate all hit this corridor at once. Projects that fix only one lever—only a park, only a village plan, only plastic cleanups—usually stall once the rest of the system pushes back. We use a ridge-to-reef analog for freshwater: catchment-aware, corridor-wide, and honest about tenure and politics, not “the river is just a blue line on a map.”

Landscape diagnostics mix mapping, threat triage, seasonal hydrology, and field checks. We focus where keystone habitat patches, flagship species, and what communities actually need line up—and we ground every layer in people on the ground, not maps for their own sake.

2. Theory of change & programmatic pillars

Our theory of change is blunt: lasting gains need stewardship capacity, incentive alignment, and accountable monitoring moving together. Patrols and planting fail if policy and money downstream still reward damage; monitoring fails if no one can act on the data.

Community co-governance pathways (clear forums, who decides what, rights-holders vs. stakeholders) sit beside nature-based solutions (NbS) chosen for shade, banks, filtration, and carbon—not hectare vanity metrics. A practical MEAL stack (monitoring, evaluation, accountability, and learning) stays lean: simple indicators first; fancier tools only when they answer a real management question.

3. Biodiversity, flagship species, & trophic signaling

We track a surrogate species ladder—herons, otters, turtles, others—as trophic signaling for how the system is doing. Methods stay modest where they must (transects, structured observation); we care about trends you can defend, not a species list for a brochure.

Restoration aims for structural heterogeneity and native mixes that fit flood-pulse dynamics—not a monoculture dressed up as “reforestation.”

4. Pollution abatement & materials stewardship

Plastic here is a waste-systems problem with a watershed footprint: schools and users, low-tech capture where crews can maintain it, and conversations with vendors and haulers at chokepoints. Clear rules on dumping and harmful burning; outreach that respects people’s time and dignity.

Circular-economy pilots only when supply, labor, and buyers line up—half-built recycling schemes burn trust fast.

5. Climate resilience, hydrosocial risk, & equitable adaptation

Climate data informs plans; it doesn’t replace local judgment. We bias toward no-regrets measures—shade and banks that help heat and floods, livelihood options that don’t depend on stripping the forest. No “resilience theater”: if there’s no money to operate and maintain it, we don’t claim a win.

6. Institutional positioning, partnerships, & finance readiness

We work with government, NGOs, universities, and sometimes business—when values match and the work is real, not logo flex. Due diligence firebreaks on partners who would cost community trust.

Blended capital means matching instrument to reality: grants for public-good layers, patient capital where revenue is plausible, catalytic money to de-risk community ventures. Carbon, if it ever appears, only under terms that survive hard questions and fair benefit-sharing.

7. Safeguarding, inclusion, & grievance sensitivity

Safeguarding is daily practice: referrals people can use, procurement that screens known bad actors, training schedules that work for caregivers and literacy levels. Grievance responsiveness matters—slow, opaque fixes fuel rumors and erode conservation gains.

8. Roadmap, KPIs, & adaptive management loops

Phased roadmap: steady local stewardship, test connectivity where it counts, lock monitoring baselines, then grow—with explicit stop rules if indicators go backwards. KPIs pair ecology with governance (who shows up, where money goes, how disputes get closed).

Quarterly learning retrospectives; annual plan updates when climate, tenure, or health realities shift. We’re not marketing a postcard—we’re funding maintenance, honesty, and trends the river would recognize.
